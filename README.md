# GTM Signal — Learn How Marketing Attribution Really Works

A free, hands-on tool for understanding one of the most gatekept topics in marketing: **where do sales actually come from, what does each channel really cost, and where should the next dollar go?**

The software that answers these questions for real companies costs hundreds of dollars a month and hides its workings behind AI. This does the opposite: it's free, it's open, and it *shows you the maths* — so you can learn the ideas by clicking around instead of reading a textbook.

> **Live demo:** _turn on GitHub Pages (Settings → Pages → deploy from `main` / root) and paste your link here._
> Or just open `index.html` in any web browser. No install, no sign-up, nothing to configure.

> ⚠️ **The numbers are made up.** They come from a realistic but entirely fictional B2B software company, generated by a script. This is a tool for *learning the concepts*, not a report on a real business.

## Who this is for

Anyone who wants to understand marketing measurement without paying for enterprise software or sitting through a course:

- marketers who keep hearing "attribution" and want to actually *get* it,
- students and career-switchers building real data literacy,
- and anyone curious about how the tools work underneath.

## What you'll learn

By playing with it, you'll come away genuinely understanding:

- **Attribution** — the surprisingly hard question of which marketing touch "earns the credit" for a sale, and why five perfectly reasonable methods can give you five different answers.
- **Unit economics** — CAC (what it costs to win a customer), LTV (what that customer is worth over time), and payback (how long until they've earned back what you spent), explained in plain language.
- **Diminishing returns** — why pouring more money into a "winning" channel eventually stops working.
- **Budget allocation** — how to reason about where the *next* dollar should go, not just where the last one went.

## The five views

1. **Executive overview** — the big picture: total spend, revenue won, what it costs to acquire a customer, how long they take to pay back, and the funnel from first lead to closed sale.
2. **Attribution lens** — the heart of the tool. Switch between five ways of giving channels credit (first-touch, last-touch, linear, time-decay, position-based) and watch the *same* revenue get split completely differently. Each method comes with a plain-English note on what it assumes and where it can mislead you.
3. **Channel economics** — a table of what each channel costs and earns, with sliders so you can change the assumptions (profit margin, customer lifetime) and see the numbers move in real time.
4. **Diminishing returns** — curves showing where each channel is still worth more money versus where it's already "full," plus an honest section on why this is a *clue*, not proof.
5. **Budget planner** — shift money from over-spent channels to under-invested ones and see the projected gain.

## Three "aha" ideas to walk away with

- **The credit changes with the lens.** The exact same revenue can make a channel look like the hero or the bystander, depending purely on which method you pick. There is no single "true" number — only different questions, each with its own bias.
- **Showing up on a winning journey isn't the same as causing the win.** A channel can collect loads of credit and still not be *adding* much — which is exactly why the diminishing-returns view exists as a reality check.
- **"Sourced" and "attributed" can disagree.** Two perfectly honest reports can rank the same channels differently. That gap is why marketing and finance so often end up arguing about the same data.

The full reasoning behind every calculation — and the honest limits of each method — lives in **[`docs/methodology.md`](docs/methodology.md)**.

## How to use it

Open `index.html` in a web browser (or visit the live link). Click the buttons, drag the sliders, and watch everything respond instantly. There's nothing to break — refresh the page to reset.

## How it works (the simple version)

It's three small parts:

1. **The data** — a short Python script invents a realistic fictional company's marketing numbers (`scripts/generate_data.py`).
2. **The maths** — a small set of rules splits the credit across channels and works out the economics. This runs right inside your browser, which is why changes are instant.
3. **The page** — turns those numbers into the interactive charts you see (`index.html`).

You never have to read a line of code to use the tool — but it's all open if you want to look under the hood.

## Rebuild it yourself (optional)

```bash
python scripts/generate_data.py   # creates the fictional dataset
python scripts/build.py           # places the data inside index.html
# then open index.html in a browser
```

No extra software is required beyond Python (standard library only).
